# Equity for Scientists

**Your professor wants 30%. Your co-founder wants 50%. Here's the framework that actually works.**

## The Situation

You're a researcher. You built something in a lab. Now you want to start a company. Immediately, three people show up with their hands out:

1. **Your university** — "We own the IP."
2. **Your advisor/professor** — "I supervised the research."
3. **Your 'business co-founder'** — "I'll handle everything you don't want to."

All three will try to take too much. Here's how to handle each.

## The University IP Question

### How it works (generally)

Most universities claim ownership of IP created using their resources. This is true in the US, China, and Europe — but the details vary enormously.

**US:** Most universities have a tech transfer office (TTO). They'll want to license the IP to your company. Typical terms: 1-3% royalty + a small equity stake (0.5-5%). Some are negotiable. Stanford, MIT, and CMU are founder-friendly. Others... less so.

**China:** University IP policies are evolving rapidly. The 2020 reforms (职务科技成果赋权) allow researchers at many universities to own or co-own their inventions. But implementation varies wildly. **Check your university's specific policy before assuming anything.**

### What to do

1. **Talk to your TTO early.** Don't build a company for 6 months and then surprise them. They will be much harder to negotiate with.
2. **If possible, rebuild the core tech outside the university.** If your company's implementation is genuinely new (not a copy of your lab code), the IP argument weakens significantly.
3. **Never sign an exclusive license back to the university.** Non-exclusive is fine if you must.
4. **Budget 2-6 months** for this process. It's slow. Start early.

## The Professor / Advisor

### The pitch you'll hear

"I mentored you for 4 years. I introduced you to investors. I should be a co-founder."

### The reality

Your professor is almost certainly not going to quit their tenured position to work on your startup full-time. And a co-founder who isn't full-time is not a co-founder — they're an advisor.

### Fair terms

| Role | Equity | Vesting |
|---|---|---|
| Full-time co-founder (quits academia) | 15-30% | 4 years, 1-year cliff |
| Part-time technical advisor, heavy involvement | 1-3% | 2 years |
| Light advisor, occasional introductions | 0.25-1% | 2 years |
| "I supervised the research" (no ongoing involvement) | 0% (but maybe a small advisory grant) | — |

### How to have the conversation

Be respectful but direct: *"I deeply value your mentorship. For the company to succeed, I need to reserve equity for the team that's building it full-time. I'd love for you to be an advisor — here's what that looks like."*

If they push for more: *"I've talked to [MiraclePlus / investors / other founders] and this is the standard range for advisor roles. Taking more would make it very hard to raise funding."*

## The Business Co-Founder

### The pitch you'll hear

"You build the tech, I'll handle everything else — fundraising, sales, hiring, ops."

### The reality check

"Everything else" is vague. And in a technical company, the technical founder is the CEO more often than not. Before giving someone 40-50% of your company, ask:

1. **Have they done this before?** First-time "business co-founders" are often less useful than they think.
2. **Can I hire for this role in 12 months?** If yes, they shouldn't get co-founder equity.
3. **Are they willing to commit full-time, immediately, with no salary?** If not, they're not a co-founder.

### Fair terms

| Situation | Split |
|---|---|
| Both co-founders are equally critical, both full-time | 50/50 |
| Technical founder is clearly the visionary, business co-founder executes | 60/40 or 65/35 |
| Technical founder + later-stage business hire | 70/30 with aggressive vesting |
| "Business co-founder" who only does intros | Don't give equity. Pay a fee. |

### The Golden Rule

**Everyone vests.** 4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff. No exceptions. Not for you, not for your co-founder, not for your professor. If someone refuses to vest, they're telling you they plan to leave early.

## The China-Specific Angle

- **Guanxi (关系) is overvalued in equity discussions.** "I know the right people" is not worth 20% of your company. Introductions depreciate. Technology compounds.
- **Be careful with "hidden shareholders" (代持).** It's common in China for someone to hold equity on behalf of another. This creates legal risk. If your cap table has proxies, clean it up before raising institutional money.
- **VIE structures** matter if you're doing anything with foreign investment. Get a lawyer who specifically handles VIE for tech startups. Don't use your uncle's corporate lawyer.

## Quick Decision Tree

```
Is this person working full-time on the company?
├── No → They are an ADVISOR (0.25-3%), not a co-founder
└── Yes →
    Is their contribution irreplaceable?
    ├── No → They are an early EMPLOYEE (1-5% + salary)
    └── Yes →
        Are they taking equal risk (no salary, full commitment)?
        ├── No → Adjusted co-founder equity (20-35%)
        └── Yes → Equal or near-equal co-founder (40-50%)
```

## The One Thing to Remember

Equity disputes kill more startups than bad products. Have the hard conversation on Day 1. Put it in writing. Use vesting. If someone is offended by standard terms, they were going to be a problem anyway.
